Notes on Methods:
- The researchers look at how the loyalty program changed customer behavior in three areas:
visit frequency, spending per visit, and six-month hiatus rate.

- This early analysis is meant to show that the changes they later model mathematically are already visible in the raw data‚Äînot just an artifact of their model.
- The modeling framework they will use later focuses on visit frequency and attrition, not spending, because they found the program had almost no effect on spending.
- To measure the program‚Äôs true impact, they need a proper control group, since membership wasn‚Äôt randomly assigned.
- Even customers who were automatically enrolled differed from non-members before the loyalty program started.
‚Üí So selection bias must be addressed.
- They use three strategies to deal with these selection effects (the paragraph summarizes the first one):

Notes on Visit Frequency
- Visit Frequency (how often customers come back)
- The researchers measured visit frequency by looking at the number of days between visits.
‚Üí Fewer days between visits = higher visit frequency.
- A histogram shows most customers return 3‚Äì10 weeks after their previous visit.
- For descriptive purposes, they only analyze customers who return within 182 days (six months) to avoid issues with right-censored data.
- Using three different regression methods, they find consistent results:
- The loyalty program reduces days between visits by ~2.3‚Äì2.9 days.
- This equals about a 5‚Äì6% increase in visit frequency.

## Key Effect Sizes and Significance from Table 4:

### **Days Between Visits (Primary Outcome 1):**
- Effect Size: -2.9 to -2.3 days reduction
- Significance:(p<0.001 to p<0.05)
- Practical Impact: 5-6% increase in visit frequency

### **Six-Month Hiatus Prevention (Primary Outcome 2):**
- Effect Size:** -0.32 to -0.27 (logistic regression coefficients)
- Significance:(p<0.001) across all models - **Most robust finding**
- Practical Impact: Major reduction in customer churn (drives 29.5% CLV increase)

### **Statistical Significance Levels:**
-p<0.001** (highly significant) 
-p<0.01** (very significant)
-p<0.05** (significant)

### **Key Insights:**
1. **Six-month hiatus reduction** shows the strongest and most consistent effects (*** across all models)
2. **Visit frequency improvements** are moderate but meaningful (2-3 day reduction)
3. **Results are robust** across different analytical methods (segment fixed effects, individual fixed effects, propensity score matching)
4. **Large sample sizes** (12K-19K observations) provide strong statistical power
5. **The hiatus prevention effect is the primary driver** of the 29.5% CLV increase mentioned earlier

The most important finding is that loyalty programs significantly reduce long-term customer churn
 (six-month hiatus), which has much larger business impact than the modest improvements in visit frequency.

### Why the Effect Size is Major:

**1. Statistical Translation:**
- Coefficients (-0.32 to -0.27) = 25-30% reduction in churn odds
- Baseline: Non-members have 24% six-month hiatus rate
- Program impact: Reduces hiatus rate to ~15-18%

**2. Business Value Context:**
- Acquiring new customers costs 5-25x more than retention
- Six-month hiatus often becomes permanent customer loss
- Effect is highly significant (p<0.001) across all analytical methods

**3. Financial Impact:**
- Hiatus prevention drives the 29.5% Customer Lifetime Value increase
- Preventing churn has exponential value over time


### Key Findings

#### Primary Impact: Customer Attrition Reduction
- **Major attrition reduction**: 5.4 percentage point drop
- **Baseline context**: Without program, attrition would be 23.3%
- **Relative improvement**: 23% reduction in customer attrition

#### Segmentation Effects: Program Impact Varies by Customer Type
**Highest Impact Segments:**
- Very infrequent customers (1 pre-program visit): 7.2 percentage point attrition drop
- Very frequent customers (6+ pre-program visits): 5.2 percentage point attrition drop

**Lower Impact:**
- Moderate-frequency customers (2-5 prior visits): minimal or non-significant effects

#### Secondary Impact: Visit Frequency
- **Modest increase**: 5-6% more frequent visits
- **Practical measure**: 2.3-2.9 fewer days between visits
- **Note**: Frequency effects are small compared to attrition effects

#### Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) Results
**Overall Pattern:**
- CLV increases for all segments over 5-year (260-week) horizon
- **U-shaped impact curve**: Biggest gains for least frequent AND most frequent customers

**Primary Driver:**
- **Attrition reduction explains majority of CLV lift**
- Visit frequency changes contribute only small portion of total value increase

## Model Framework: Dynamic Customer Decision-Making

### 1. Purpose of the Model
- Simulates customer decision-making under dynamic incentives, like loyalty programs
- Accounts for both immediate utility (current purchase benefits) and future utility (expected rewards)
- Customers choose among mutually exclusive options (e.g., no purchase, small, medium, or large orders) over discrete time periods

### 2. Dynamic Decision-Making
- Customers can act myopically (single-period focus) or dynamically (multiple-period perspective)
- A positive discount factor (Œ±) indicates that customers weigh future rewards in current decisions
- Future expected benefits are influenced by state variables like cumulative spending, time since last purchase, and pricing environment

### 3. State Variables
- **Cumulative purchases (Cum):** Tracks total spending to determine reward eligibility
- **Loyalty rewards (L):** Indicates if a customer crosses a reward threshold (e.g., $1,000, $1,500, $2,000)
- **Recency/frequency indicators (Rcn, FM):** Capture prior purchasing behavior to reflect experience and transaction costs
- **Duration (Dur):** Time since last purchase; can indicate inventory effects or customer attrition

### 4. Marketing Mix & Incentives
- **Shipping fees:** Different schedules (e.g., free shipping over $75, graduated fees) influence purchase size
- **Pricing:** Modeled using the average price of top-selling items; expected future prices affect customer decisions
- **Promotions:** E-mail coupons or discounts affect immediate utility

### Key Insights for Your Situation
**Research collectively shows that loyalty programs are highly effective for reducing customer churn in service industries, with strongest effects on customers at behavioral extremes.**

#### Problem-Related Insights
- Customer attrition is a significant issue affecting 17-24% of service customers annually

- Beauty service businesses face similar retention challenges as other appointment-based service providers

#### Solution-Related Insights
- Non-tiered loyalty programs can increase CLV by 23-30% through attrition reduction, not increased spending
- Programs work primarily through psychological mechanisms (feeling valued) rather than economic incentives
- Only 2% redemption rates still yield significant retention benefits, proving relationship-building matters more than reward generosity
- Simple program structures (email signup + spending thresholds) are as effective as complex tiered systems
- Brand attachment and brand love are the strongest loyalty drivers, suggesting emotional connection is key

#### Implementation Considerations
- Target extreme segments: very infrequent customers (highest improvement potential) and very frequent customers (highest absolute value)
- Keep program simple: email collection + spending tracking + basic rewards (e.g., $5 off every $100)
- Focus on relationship-building messaging over transactional benefits
- Implement low-cost technology solutions for small businesses
- Set realistic thresholds achievable within typical customer visit patterns (5-6 visits for beauty services)
- Consider pricing sensitivity - high service prices may reduce program attractiveness
